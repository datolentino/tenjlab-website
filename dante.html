01 About

I am an Assistant Professor at the UCLA Joe C. Wen School of Nursing and the founder and director of the TenJ Lab. My research portfolio is built on three connected pillars: diabetes, biobehavioral stress, and digital health. I study how cardiovascular-kidney-metabolic (CKM) conditions, such as type 2 diabetes, emerge from the intersection of biological, behavioral, and social processes.

Much of my current work focuses on Filipino and Asian Americans, who face a higher risk of type 2 diabetes, poorer health outcomes, and lower engagement in optimal self-care than other Asian American groups. To help explain these patterns, I examine mechanisms such as colonial mentality (the internalization of colonial attitudes), allostatic load (the biological burden of chronic stress), and financial toxicity, alongside applications of digital health and artificial intelligence.

My professional background spans clinical and informatics roles. I completed my PhD at the University of Arizona and a postdoctoral fellowship in health services research at the University of Michigan through the National Clinician Scholars Program, and I am a Fellow of the American Medical Informatics Association.

02 Research

Three interconnected programs

01

Diabetes and metabolic health

Community-partnered, culturally grounded research on type 2 diabetes and related cardiovascular-kidney-metabolic conditions among Filipino and Asian Americans. Rooted in Sikolohiyang Pilipino, this work pairs qualitative inquiry with spatial mapping of diabetes education programs across Los Angeles and Orange counties.

02

Biobehavioral stress mechanisms

A stress-science approach that links biological, behavioral, and social processes to metabolic health. This work pairs validated psychosocial measures, including colonial mentality, with biomarkers such as allostatic load, building toward a biopsychosocial model linking chronic stress to type 2 diabetes.

03

Informatics and digital health

Grounded in clinical informatics and human-computer interaction, this work studies nursing workflows within the electronic health record and explores how digital health tools, including wearables and artificial intelligence, can better serve patients and communities.

05 Teaching & mentoring

Education as an agent of mobility

I teach doctoral methods and informatics grounded in the nursing metaparadigm of person, environment, health, and nursing. I treat teaching as a partnership in which students co-create knowledge from their clinical and lived experience, and I build predictable routines, a wide range of readings, and regular check-ins so every student can do their best work.

Mentoring is central to my program. I have mentored undergraduate, master’s, and doctoral students, served on doctoral committees including as chair, and co-authored publications and presentations with mentees, building a strong and independent training pipeline.
