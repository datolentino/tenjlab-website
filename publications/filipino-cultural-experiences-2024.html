Filipino Americans’ social and cultural experiences of type 2 diabetes management: Cultural paradox, ownership, and success definition.

Tolentino, D. A., & Byrnes, M. (2024).

Journal of Transcultural Nursing. · doi:10.1177/10436596231209041

In plain language

We talked with Filipino Americans about what living with and managing type 2 diabetes is really like day to day: how family, food, faith, and cultural expectations can either help or get in the way. The goal is care that respects and works with their culture.

About this study

Filipino Americans are one of the largest Asian American subgroups in the United States, yet their experiences living with type 2 diabetes have rarely been studied in depth. In this qualitative study, the team listened closely to how Filipino Americans manage the condition in everyday life.

Three threads ran through their stories. First, a cultural paradox: the very things that anchor Filipino life, such as food, family, and gatherings, can both support and complicate diabetes management. Second, a strong sense of ownership, as participants described taking personal responsibility for their health and care. Third, a personal definition of success, measured not only by clinical numbers but by each person’s own goals and values.

Together, these findings point toward care that works with culture rather than against it, that respects patients’ agency, and that honors how each person defines living well with diabetes.

Key themes

  • Cultural paradox: family, food, and tradition as both support and challenge
  • Ownership: personal responsibility for health and care decisions
  • Defining success on one’s own terms, beyond clinical metrics
